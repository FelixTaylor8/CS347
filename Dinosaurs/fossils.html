Famous Fossils

Bones and Casts

Fighting Dinosaurs

Discovered: Mongolia, 1971
Resides: Central Museum of Mongolia
Dinosaur(s): Protoceratops
Velociraptor
Age: 80 million years (Cretaceous)
Source: Wikipedia

Protoceratops bit down on the arm of veliciraptor and never let go! This fossil is famous for giving us a glimpse into these animals' behavior.

Image source

Sue

Discovered: South Dakota, 1990
Resides: The Field Museum of Natural History (Chicago)
Dinosaur(s): Tyrannosaurus
Age: 67 million years (Cretaceous)
Source: Wikipedia

Named after the paleontologist who discovered it, Sue is "one of the largest, most extensive, and best preserved Tyrannosaurus rex specimens ever found." Its discovery and subsequent legal battles are detailed in the documentary Dinosaur 13.

Image source

Maximo

Discovered: Argentina
Resides: The Field Museum of Natural History (Chicago)
Dinosaur(s): Patagotitan
Age: 100 million years (Cretaceous)
Source: Field Museum

This is a composite cast of many different Patagotitans, famous for being the largest dinosaur species discovered at 28 feet tall and 70 tons.

Image source

Suncor nodosaur

Discovered: Alberta, 2011
Resides: Royal Tyrrell Museum of Palaeontology (Alberta)
Dinosaur(s): Borealopelta
Age: 110 million years (Cretaceous)
Source: Wikipedia

After dying and ending up in a river, the elements preserved this fossil so well that it almost appears to be sleeping. Paleontologists have been able to determine what color its scales were and what was in its stomach when it died.

Image source

Sinosauropteryx

Discovered: China, 1996
Resides: Inner Mongolia Museum
Dinosaur(s): Sinosauropteryx
Age: 124 million years (Cretaceous)
Source: Wikipedia

This was one of the first fossils to establish that non-avian dinosaurs had feathers. This one's were ginger and banded, like a tabby cat.

Image source

Berlin Archaeopteryx

Discovered: Berlin, ~1874
Resides: Natural History Museum of Berlin
Dinosaur(s): Archaeopteryx
Age: 150 million years (Jurassic)
Source: Wikipedia

Said to be the missing link between dinosaurs and birds, this fossil has clear imprints of wings that allowed it to fly. There are only around 12 Archaeopteryx fossils known.

Image source

Hatcher

Discovered: 1888-1891, US
Resides: Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History
Dinosaur(s): Triceratops
Age: 66 million years (Cretaceous)
Source: Smithsonian
Wikipedia

Hatcher is the first Triceratops fossil ever displayed. Like Maximo, this fossil was built from compositing many different specimens. The original Hatcher was awkward-looking due to its unproportional parts, but in 1996 it was replaced with an appropriately-sized cast. Today, Hatcher is being eaten by the Smithsonian's new T. rex!
